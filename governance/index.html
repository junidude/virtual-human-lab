Governance

Virtual Human Lab is an independent, unincorporated, non-commercial academic association organized in the Republic of Korea. It is governed by its Korean-language Bylaws, voting Regular Members, a General Assembly, and an elected Representative.

VHL is not an official unit of any government agency, military organization, university, hospital, or company. Professional affiliations identify individuals only and do not imply institutional sponsorship, endorsement, approval, or responsibility.

Association evidence

The public structure tracks the substance, not a label.

VHL records the elements that distinguish a continuing member association from a loose project team: a shared academic purpose, association-style bylaws, decision-making organs, representative authority, majority voting, member continuity, and separate asset management.

Purpose

Defined academic mission

VHL works at the intersection of medicine, transcriptomics, bioinformatics, computational biology, and machine learning.

Rules

Association-style Bylaws

The Bylaws define name, purpose, members, General Assembly, Representative, voting, records, finance, research integrity, and dissolution.

Organs

General Assembly and officers

Voting Regular Members form the highest decision-making body. The Representative, Finance Officer, and Auditor operate within the Bylaws.

Voting

Majority and supermajority rules

Ordinary matters use quorum and majority rules. Bylaw changes, officer removal, expulsion, major agreements, and dissolution require higher thresholds.

Continuity

Membership can change without ending VHL

Admission, withdrawal, replacement, or loss of status of a member does not dissolve the association or transfer its records, accounts, domain, or assets.

Assets

Separate, non-distribution property

Funds and assets are used only for academic purposes. No surplus or residual asset is distributed to members, officers, advisors, or related persons.

Official professional profile

The Representative implements General Assembly resolutions, manages official communications, and represents VHL only within the authority granted by the Bylaws.

  • Material funding, data-access, intellectual-property, debt, and institutional agreements require collective approval.
  • No officer may imply that a member's employer, hospital, university, company, government agency, or military organization sponsors VHL without written authorization.
  • Advisors and project collaborators provide expertise but do not hold voting or representative authority unless separately admitted as Regular Members.

Operating principles

The governance model is built to be auditable.

Meetings

Minutes and records

General Assembly and material committee meetings are recorded internally with agenda, attendance, votes, conflict recusals, and resolutions.

Conflicts

Disclosure and recusal

Members with direct financial, employment, authorship, intellectual-property, disciplinary, or related conflicts disclose and do not vote on that matter.

Support

Documented terms

Money, cloud credits, GPU access, equipment, and data access are accepted only under documented terms that preserve scientific independence and compliance obligations.

Dissolution

No private residual distribution

After lawful liabilities and record duties are settled, remaining assets go to a non-profit academic, scientific, medical-education, or public-interest organization.

Governing documents

Public copy, private originals.

The public pages exclude signatures, private addresses, full meeting minutes, account information, personal records, and protected research information.

What this page does not do

This website does not certify VHL's legal status, replace signed founding records, substitute for tax registration, or create institutional sponsorship. The relevant evidence remains the adopted Bylaws, founding General Assembly records, Representative acceptance, member records, accounting separation, and actual operation over time.

Public legal context

Korean court practice distinguishes a loose partnership-like project from an unincorporated association by looking at the strength of the organization: purpose, association-style rules, decision-making and representative organs, majority-based operation, continuity despite member changes, and defined rules for representation, meetings, capital, and property management.

See also the Korean Civil Procedure Act Article 52, which recognizes litigation capacity for an unincorporated association or foundation with a representative or manager.
